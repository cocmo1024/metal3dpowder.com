IN718 is one of the most practical nickel superalloy routes in AM when the application is heat-driven, mechanically demanding, and too geometrically difficult to leave to simpler alloys. It stays commercially relevant because many high-temperature parts need a route that is stronger and more durable than stainless steel but more mature and accessible than the most specialized superalloys.

## Where IN718 is strongest

IN718 usually deserves attention when the application is tied to:

- turbine-adjacent hardware
- exhaust-side structures
- hot-section brackets and supports
- temperature-driven industrial AM parts

These are the kinds of parts where geometry freedom and heat resistance can combine into a real AM value proposition.

## Why the application matters more than the alloy reputation

IN718 is a familiar name, but that alone is not the reason to choose it. The right question is whether the part is actually a high-temperature structural problem that needs a nickel route.

If the service condition is only mildly elevated or the geometry is easy to produce conventionally, IN718 may be more material than the application really needs.

## When IN718 is a better fit than lower-temperature alloys

Buyers often move toward IN718 when the application begins to exceed what stainless or lower-temperature tool steels can handle comfortably. The application is usually not just "hot." It is hot plus mechanically meaningful plus geometrically demanding.

That combination is why IN718 remains one of the most credible first nickel routes to review for AM hot-section parts.

## The practical takeaway

IN718 is strongest when the part is a real hot-section or high-temperature structural problem, not just a general industrial metal part. If the geometry and service temperature both matter, IN718 usually earns its place early in the material review.
