Hastelloy X is a nickel alloy typically considered when oxidation resistance and high-temperature stability become central to the design. In additive manufacturing, it is not the default first nickel powder. Buyers usually reach it when a standard corrosion-oriented alloy or a structural workhorse like IN718 no longer fits the hot-gas environment well enough.

On this site, Hastelloy X starts from **USD 79/kg**, which is slightly above IN718 and IN625. That is commercially reasonable for a more specialized hot-section-oriented alloy.

## Where Hastelloy X enters the conversation

Hastelloy X is usually relevant for:

- combustion hardware
- hot-gas flow-path components
- oxidation-driven service
- high-temperature industrial parts

The reason it matters is simple: some AM jobs are not mainly about corrosion, and they are not only about structural strength either. They are about surviving a hot oxidizing environment without choosing the wrong nickel alloy by habit.

## How it differs from IN718 and IN625

Compared with IN718, Hastelloy X is usually chosen less for peak structural strength and more for hot-environment durability and oxidation-focused service. Compared with IN625, it is more specifically tied to higher-temperature gas-path thinking rather than general corrosion-oriented nickel-alloy use.

That is why buyers should not request it casually. The alloy choice should be supported by the operating environment.

If the project is mainly structural, start with [IN718 powder](/posts/Alloys/gh4169-in718-powder/). If the job is mainly corrosion-driven, start with [IN625 powder](/posts/Alloys/gh3625-in625-powder/). If the problem is combustion-adjacent hot service, Hastelloy X deserves a serious look.

## Why procurement still needs detail

Specialized alloys punish vague RFQs. A supplier can quote Hastelloy X generically, but the technical usefulness of the offer depends on whether the buyer states:

- service environment
- AM process
- PSD range
- document requirements
- order quantity
- target delivery window

This matters because a development build for internal evaluation is very different from a controlled hardware program with release documents and export logistics.

## What to check before buying

With Hastelloy X, buyers should be explicit about the thermal problem. If the environment is only moderately hot but corrosion-heavy, another nickel alloy may be the cleaner answer. If the environment is oxidation-driven and hot-section-oriented, Hastelloy X can be a stronger technical fit than defaulting to a more common alloy.

That is the real buying discipline: selecting the alloy that matches the failure mode instead of selecting the alloy that happens to be more famous.

## The practical takeaway

Use Hastelloy X when the AM part belongs to an oxidation-resistant, high-temperature, combustion-oriented problem. Do not treat it as a generic premium nickel upgrade. The right comparison is against the environment, then against [IN718 vs IN625](/posts/Alloys/in718-vs-in625-powder/) if the project still sits between structural and corrosion-driven nickel-alloy routes.
