IN718 and IN625 are both nickel-based AM powders, but they solve different engineering problems. Buyers sometimes group them together because both are common laser powder bed and DED alloys. That shortcut creates avoidable sourcing mistakes. The better question is not which alloy is more advanced. The better question is what kind of part the project is actually trying to build.

IN718 is usually chosen when the job needs high strength at elevated temperature and a qualification path that is already familiar to aerospace, energy, or demanding industrial teams. IN625 is usually chosen when corrosion resistance, broader chemical exposure tolerance, or a more forgiving engineering route matters more than peak strength.

## The simplest decision rule

If the part is primarily structural and the temperature duty is serious, buyers usually start with IN718. If the part is more corrosion-driven, ducting-oriented, marine/chemical-facing, or needs nickel-alloy durability without the same strength target, buyers often start with IN625.

That is not a universal rule, but it is the right first screen.

## Where IN718 normally wins

IN718 remains one of the default nickel AM alloys because it combines familiarity with high-temperature structural credibility. Teams choose it for brackets, housings, energy hardware, and other components where strength retention matters more than pure corrosion logic.

On this site, IN718 is listed from **USD 76/kg**, which reflects its position as a mainstream but still high-value nickel superalloy.

IN718 is usually the better fit when the buying conversation includes:

- structural load
- elevated-temperature duty
- established qualification practice
- tighter mechanical-property targets

For that reason, IN718 is often the safer answer when a project needs a nickel alloy and the final application is still not fully settled. It gives engineers a more structural starting point than IN625.

## Where IN625 normally wins

IN625 is usually the better answer when corrosion exposure is the first design driver. It is commonly considered for chemical equipment, marine-adjacent use, heat-management hardware, and aggressive-service parts where the team wants nickel-alloy durability without centering the decision on maximum strength.

On this site, IN625 is listed from **USD 71/kg**, which makes it slightly lower in starting price than IN718. That should not be interpreted as simply "cheaper is better." It reflects different alloy logic and different end-use expectations.

IN625 is often the better fit when the RFQ is driven by:

- corrosion resistance
- broad media exposure
- thermal or flow-path hardware
- lower emphasis on peak age-hardened strength

## Why the wrong comparison wastes time

Many RFQs use the phrase "nickel alloy powder" without explaining the service logic. That creates unnecessary back-and-forth because IN718 and IN625 are not interchangeable catalog lines. A supplier can quote both, but the useful answer depends on whether the real target is strength, corrosion, thermal service, or qualification familiarity.

If the buyer does not state that clearly, the first quote may be commercially valid but technically weak. That is one of the easiest ways for an inquiry cycle to lose momentum.

## Powder details that still matter for both alloys

The alloy choice is only the first layer. Buyers still need to define:

- process route
- PSD window
- oxygen and chemistry priorities
- document package
- packaging format
- quantity and ship-to country

For nickel alloys, this matters because the quote can move meaningfully once the documentation and release expectations are clear. A prototype inquiry and a qualification-oriented production inquiry rarely price the same way.

## When Hastelloy X enters the conversation

Some teams compare IN718 and IN625, then realize the part is actually a better fit for a more combustion- or oxidation-oriented alloy. In that case, the decision may need to expand to [Hastelloy X powder](/posts/Alloys/hastelloy-x-powder/), especially for hot-gas or combustor-adjacent hardware.

That does not mean Hastelloy X replaces both alloys. It means the nickel-alloy decision should be tied to the operating environment instead of habit.

## How buyers should frame the RFQ

If the project is undecided, the RFQ should say what matters most:

1. structural performance at temperature
2. corrosion resistance
3. qualification familiarity
4. manufacturability and lead time

That gives the supplier enough context to recommend the right nickel alloy instead of mechanically quoting both.

## The practical takeaway

Choose IN718 when the part is structurally demanding and elevated-temperature mechanical performance sits at the center of the decision. Choose IN625 when the part is driven more by corrosion resistance, service environment, and practical nickel-alloy durability. If the real requirement is hot-section oxidation resistance, widen the comparison instead of forcing the wrong two-alloy choice.
