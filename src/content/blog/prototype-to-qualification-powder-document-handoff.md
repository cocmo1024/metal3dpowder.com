The document problem in AM sourcing usually appears at the program transition point. Prototype work can tolerate informal email history and evolving assumptions. Qualification cannot. When a project moves toward controlled release, the powder record has to move with it.

That handoff is not only about filing certificates. It is about making sure the next powder order, the incoming inspection step, and the internal approval logic all reference the same requirement set.

## What usually gets lost during the transition

The most common gaps are:

- RFQ assumptions that were never frozen
- certificate expectations that changed but were not documented
- traceability logic that exists in email but not in the release file
- package and handling rules that were assumed rather than stated
- reuse or lot-management expectations that were never written down

These gaps are manageable early on. They become expensive once the project starts treating powder supply as part of a qualification path.

## What the handoff package should include

A practical prototype-to-qualification handoff usually needs:

- the controlled alloy and process definition
- PSD and chemistry expectations
- certificate and traceability requirements
- incoming inspection basis
- packaging and storage rules
- any change-notification expectations

That does not mean every project needs a heavy regulated document stack. It means the release basis must be clear enough that the next order can be judged against it.

## Supplier onboarding should be part of the handoff

If the program is still working with a new source, the handoff should also show how the supplier was initially evaluated. That is why a structured [first article sample request](/posts/Alloys/first-article-sample-request-new-powder-suppliers/) matters. It creates the first documented bridge between prototype learning and controlled sourcing.

## Qualification requires a release basis, not just more paperwork

Many teams react to qualification by asking for "more documents." That is incomplete. The real question is whether the documents point to a release basis the team can actually use. For powder lots, that usually means:

- the certificate pack says what the project needs it to say
- the incoming inspection team knows what to verify
- the buyer knows what would trigger an escalation
- the supplier knows what cannot change silently

The handoff only works if those four views align.

## Regulated and controlled sectors need earlier clarity

If the project touches medical, dental, aerospace, or similarly controlled end use, the handoff discipline should tighten earlier rather than later. That is where the existing [medical and dental documentation guide](/posts/Alloys/medical-dental-powder-documentation-guide/) and [aerospace qualification checklist](/posts/Alloys/aerospace-powder-qualification-checklist/) become useful supporting references.

## The practical takeaway

Prototype sourcing and qualification sourcing should not live in different document realities. A clean handoff turns early learning into a controlled basis for the next order, the next receiving event, and the next supplier discussion. If the program cannot explain what changed between prototype and qualification, the powder documentation is not yet mature enough.
