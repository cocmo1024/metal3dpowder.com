An additive manufacturing powder supplier should be evaluated by more than a grade list. The supplier must match the alloy, process, particle-size distribution, chemistry controls, documentation scope, packaging, export path, and application risk.

Most poor supplier comparisons happen because the buyer compares prices before comparing specifications. Two quotes for "Ti64 powder" or "316L powder" may not describe the same powder, process window, documentation package, or supply risk.

This guide gives buyers a practical shortlist method for LPBF, EBM, DED, binder jet, MIM, and adjacent powder-based manufacturing routes.

## Quick answer

Choose a supplier only after you can answer four questions:

1. Does the powder match the alloy and AM process?
2. Does the PSD and morphology match the machine and part risk?
3. Can the supplier provide the documents and lot traceability required for the project stage?
4. Does the quote state packaging, lead time, destination, assumptions, and exclusions clearly?

If any answer is missing, the cheaper quote may simply be the less-defined quote.

## Start with the application, not the catalog

The same alloy can be used for very different applications. That changes supplier fit.

| Application context | What changes in supplier evaluation |
| --- | --- |
| Research sample | Flexibility, small quantity, clear basic chemistry, fast shipment. |
| Prototype build | PSD fit, machine compatibility, handling guidance, reasonable documentation. |
| Qualification build | Lot traceability, repeatability, chemistry control, test reports, change control. |
| Production supply | recurring lots, packaging discipline, stable lead time, audit-friendly documents. |
| High-risk parts | tighter chemistry, oxygen control, morphology review, reuse plan, stronger records. |

A supplier that is fine for a research bottle may not be acceptable for a recurring production program. A supplier that can support production may not be the fastest source for a small experimental order.

## The first supplier filter: alloy and process fit

Do not shortlist a supplier until the alloy and process are clear.

Common examples:

- Ti64, Grade 23, Grade 2, or TA15 for titanium routes;
- 316L or 17-4PH for stainless routes;
- IN718, IN625, or Hastelloy X for nickel alloy routes;
- AlSi10Mg for aluminum LPBF routes;
- CuCrZr or other copper alloys for thermal and electrical parts;
- H13 or M300 for tooling applications;
- CoCrMo for wear, dental, or medical-adjacent projects.

Then state whether the powder is intended for LPBF, EBM, DED, binder jet, MIM, thermal spray, or another process. A supplier should not treat those as interchangeable.

## The second filter: PSD and morphology

Particle-size distribution and morphology determine whether the powder can feed, spread, pack, and melt consistently.

Ask for:

- nominal PSD range;
- D10, D50, and D90 values where available;
- fines and oversize control;
- apparent density or tap density where relevant;
- flow data where relevant;
- morphology expectations such as spherical powder, satellite level, and internal porosity;
- whether the PSD is standard stock or custom cut.

For LPBF buyers, common ranges such as 15-45 um or 15-53 um may appear in many quotes, but the actual distribution curve and morphology still matter. For DED, EBM, binder jet, or MIM, do not reuse LPBF assumptions automatically.

## The third filter: chemistry and interstitial control

Chemistry controls are especially important for titanium, nickel alloys, aluminum, and some specialty powders. Oxygen, nitrogen, carbon, and hydrogen limits can affect ductility, fatigue, process behavior, and final part acceptance.

Ask the supplier what chemistry data is included and whether the report is tied to the quoted lot. If the project is qualification-sensitive, the answer should be specific, not "standard quality."

## The fourth filter: documents and traceability

Documentation needs depend on the project stage.

| Document | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Certificate of analysis | Confirms chemistry and basic lot identity. |
| PSD report | Confirms particle-size distribution for process fit. |
| SDS | Supports handling, safety, and internal approval. |
| Lot number and batch record | Supports traceability and reuse control. |
| Packaging information | Confirms how powder will be protected and handled. |
| Morphology image | Useful for higher-risk AM process reviews. |
| Oxygen or interstitial data | Critical for reactive alloys and qualification work. |

Do not demand every document for every small order. But if the part is expensive or qualification-sensitive, define the document package before price comparison.

## The fifth filter: packaging, shipping, and lead time

Powder quality can be damaged by poor packaging or handling. The quote should state package size, package type, whether inert or vacuum packaging is used when needed, shipping destination, and lead-time assumptions.

Lead time depends on:

- stock status;
- alloy family;
- PSD cut;
- test-report scope;
- packaging;
- export paperwork;
- destination country;
- whether the order is a sample, pilot lot, or recurring supply.

For schedule-sensitive projects, send the required delivery date before quotation. A nominal lead time without destination and document scope is not reliable.

## Red flags in supplier responses

Be careful when a supplier:

- quotes only by alloy name with no PSD or process discussion;
- cannot say whether powder is for LPBF, EBM, DED, binder jet, or MIM;
- avoids chemistry or oxygen-limit questions;
- cannot state document scope;
- gives a price without quantity, packaging, or destination;
- uses broad claims instead of application-specific answers;
- cannot explain whether the powder is stock or made to order;
- treats prototype, qualification, and production supply as the same situation.

One red flag does not automatically disqualify a supplier, but it tells the buyer where the hidden risk is.

## Supplier shortlist template

Use this scoring structure for a first shortlist.

| Shortlist criterion | Good answer |
| --- | --- |
| Alloy fit | Exact grade, chemistry basis, and application awareness. |
| Process fit | Powder window tied to LPBF, EBM, DED, binder jet, MIM, or other route. |
| PSD clarity | Range plus distribution data or clear reporting plan. |
| Morphology | Supplier can discuss sphericity, satellites, and flow behavior. |
| Documentation | COA, PSD, SDS, lot traceability, and special tests are defined. |
| Packaging | Package size, sealing method, and handling assumptions are stated. |
| Lead time | Stock, production, testing, and export time are separated. |
| Commercial clarity | Price is tied to quantity, destination, Incoterms, and assumptions. |

The best supplier is not always the lowest price. It is the supplier whose offer reduces uncertainty enough for the buyer's project stage.

## What to include in the RFQ email

Send this in the first email:

- alloy grade or intended application;
- target process;
- particle-size range if known;
- quantity;
- destination country;
- documentation needed;
- packaging preference;
- printer model if relevant;
- project stage: research, prototype, qualification, or production;
- target ship date;
- any oxygen, chemistry, morphology, or traceability requirement.

If you do not know the correct powder, state the part application and process. A supplier can often recommend a starting point, but only if the application is visible.

## Practical takeaway

Compare additive manufacturing powder suppliers by application fit, powder specification, process fit, documents, packaging, lead time, and commercial clarity.
