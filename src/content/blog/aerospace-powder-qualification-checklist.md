Aerospace AM sourcing does not start with the alloy. It starts with the qualification path. That is why powder procurement for aerospace programs should be treated differently from prototype or general industrial buying. The material itself matters, but the main question is whether the powder can enter a controlled process and remain traceable through the program.

FAA guidance on powder bed fusion for aircraft engine parts and EASA's additive-manufacturing certification guidance both point in the same direction: process definition, control, and evidence matter. A powder order that cannot support that discipline is not aerospace-ready, even if the chemistry is correct.

## What the qualification checklist should cover

An aerospace-oriented powder review should define:

- exact alloy designation
- batch identity and traceability
- chemistry release data
- PSD release data
- process route and machine family
- packaging and handling controls
- reuse policy if applicable
- program stage: development, qualification, or production

These are not administrative details. They shape whether the powder can sit inside a real aerospace manufacturing file.

## Why program stage matters

Development powder and qualification powder are not always the same commercial event. A development order may prioritize speed and early learning. A qualification-stage order usually needs tighter control, clearer release evidence, and stronger alignment with the manufacturing plan.

If the buyer does not state the program stage, the first quote may be technically acceptable but commercially misaligned.

## Titanium and nickel are common, but the rule is broader

Many aerospace AM inquiries focus on titanium or nickel alloys such as [TC4 / Ti64](/posts/Alloys/tc4-ti6al4v-powder/) and [IN718](/posts/Alloys/gh4169-in718-powder/). That is normal. But the qualification logic is bigger than a single alloy family. The powder file needs to support process control regardless of whether the program is titanium, nickel, or another aerospace material route.

## Where buyers should be explicit

The RFQ should state:

- whether the powder is for parameter development or controlled builds
- whether internal qualification testing is already defined
- whether reuse is permitted and how it is monitored
- what certificates must accompany each lot
- how the powder lot will be linked to build records

That gives the supplier a chance to quote for the real aerospace use case rather than for a generic AM powder sale.

## The practical takeaway

Aerospace AM buying should begin with qualification logic, not with catalog shopping. If the powder cannot support traceability, process definition, and release discipline, it is not the right input for the job no matter how attractive the initial price looks.
