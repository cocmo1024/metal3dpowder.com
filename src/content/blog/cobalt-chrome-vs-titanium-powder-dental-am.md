Dental AM buyers often start with the alloy question: cobalt chrome or titanium. The useful answer is not a slogan. It depends on what the lab or manufacturing team is trying to make, how the workflow is qualified, and what documentation path the supplier can support.

This is why dental powder buying should treat alloy choice and supply discipline as one decision rather than two separate conversations.

## Why cobalt chrome remains common in dental AM

Cobalt chrome stays central in many dental workflows because the route is familiar, commercially established, and often well aligned with controlled lab routines. The main [CoCrMo powder guide](/posts/Alloys/cocrmo-cocrw-powder/) explains the alloy context at the site level.

For dental buyers, the practical question is whether the lot identity, certificate scope, and repeat-order discipline are strong enough for the intended workflow.

## Why titanium enters the conversation

Titanium becomes relevant when the project or lab workflow has a real reason to prefer it. That may be driven by part requirements, finishing logic, weight considerations, or internal material policy. The key point is that titanium should be introduced because the application needs it, not because the procurement team assumes it is automatically the more advanced choice.

## The buying comparison should stay operational

When comparing cobalt chrome with titanium powder for dental AM, buyers should look at:

- machine compatibility
- lot traceability
- certificate requirements
- finishing and handling discipline
- repeat-order stability
- whether the program is trial-stage or controlled release

That comparison is usually more useful than debating alloy reputation in the abstract.

## Documentation matters either way

The alloy choice does not remove the need for disciplined documentation. Dental AM powder supply still depends on:

- clear lot identity
- certificate coherence
- package traceability
- release logic aligned to the actual workflow

The broader [medical and dental documentation guide](/posts/Alloys/medical-dental-powder-documentation-guide/) remains the right baseline, and the dedicated [dental cobalt chrome sourcing checklist](/posts/Alloys/dental-lab-cobalt-chrome-sourcing-checklist/) is still the most useful companion if cobalt chrome is the current path.

## The practical takeaway

Dental AM buyers should compare cobalt chrome and titanium as workflow choices, not just as alloy names. The better route is the one that fits the real dental program, the actual machine path, and the document discipline the supplier can sustain over repeat orders. If those conditions are not explicit, the buying decision is not mature enough yet.
