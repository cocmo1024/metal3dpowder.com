Ti64 and Grade 2 titanium powder solve different engineering problems. Buyers often compare them because both sit inside the titanium family, but the correct choice depends less on the word "titanium" and more on whether the part is strength-driven or corrosion-driven.

## Short answer

If the part needs a structural, high-specific-strength titanium route for additive manufacturing, Ti64 is usually the right starting point. If the part is chosen mainly for corrosion resistance, chemical stability, or commercially pure titanium behavior, Grade 2 is often the simpler answer.

That distinction should be made before the RFQ goes out. Otherwise suppliers end up quoting two very different titanium routes against the same vague request.

## Where Ti64 makes more sense

Ti64 is usually chosen for structural AM parts where strength-to-weight ratio matters. Aerospace brackets, lightweight engineering components, and many medical or performance-driven parts sit in this zone.

Commercially, Ti64 also benefits from broad AM familiarity. The supply chain is mature, LPBF use is common, and qualification history is deeper than with many other titanium systems. That makes it easier to justify when the project needs a strong structural story.

## Where Grade 2 makes more sense

Grade 2 is the better answer when the project is not chasing Ti64-level strength. If the part is valued for corrosion resistance, chemical service compatibility, or the logic of commercially pure titanium, Grade 2 becomes a serious candidate.

This matters because some projects ask for "titanium powder" out of habit when the real requirement is simply corrosion resistance. In that case, Ti64 may be more alloy than the application needs.

## How the powder discussion changes

For both alloys, buyers still need to define PSD, process route, quantity, and document requirements. But the technical focus shifts.

With Ti64, the conversation usually centers on structural use, chemistry control, interstitial limits, and whether the route aligns with Grade 5 or Grade 23 expectations. With Grade 2, the conversation is more likely to focus on purity, corrosion-oriented use, and whether the project really needs a commercially pure titanium route.

If the project has already decided on Ti64 but is still unsure about ELI-level control, use the direct [Grade 23 vs Grade 5 guide](/posts/Alloys/ti64-grade-23-vs-grade-5-powder/) instead of reopening the broader Grade 2 comparison.

## Price context

On this site, Ti64 is currently listed from USD 89/kg and Grade 2 from USD 82/kg. That difference is not huge, and it should not be treated as the whole decision. The more important question is whether the project benefits from Ti64-level performance or from the simpler logic of Grade 2.

If the wrong alloy is chosen, even a cheaper price becomes expensive. Qualification delay and redesign cost more than a small difference in price per kilogram.

## RFQ questions that decide the right alloy

Before asking for a quote, confirm:

- Is the part strength-driven or corrosion-driven?
- Is the route LPBF, EBM, or DED?
- Is there a target chemistry or interstitial limit?
- Is the part moving into qualification or still in early development?
- What documents are required with shipment?

If those questions are answered early, the supplier can help narrow the titanium route quickly.

## The practical takeaway

Ti64 is the safer default for structural titanium AM work. Grade 2 is the more logical choice when corrosion behavior and commercially pure titanium logic matter more than high structural performance. The right RFQ should make that distinction obvious from the first line.
