IN625 powder is often a strong nickel route when the part is a ducting problem shaped by corrosion exposure, temperature, and geometry. Marine-adjacent and chemical-service ducting usually needs more environmental durability than stainless can comfortably offer, but not always the structural emphasis of IN718.

## Where this route is usually relevant

IN625 is often reviewed for:

- marine-adjacent ducting
- chemical-process ducting
- corrosion-facing nickel manifolds
- hot-side routed hardware with aggressive media exposure

The AM value usually appears when the ducting needs internal shape freedom, consolidated routing, or part simplification.

## Why ducting often points to IN625

Ducting is frequently an environment-led problem. If the part is exposed to corrosive media or harsh service but is not a strength-first turbine structure, IN625 often lands in the right zone between stainless and hotter structural nickel routes.

## When another nickel alloy should take over

If the part is mainly structural and high-temperature-loaded, [IN718](/posts/Alloys/gh4169-in718-powder/) may be the better answer. If the environment is more oxidation-driven furnace or hot-gas service, [Hastelloy X](/posts/Alloys/hastelloy-x-powder/) may be cleaner.

## The practical takeaway

Use IN625 powder when the application is marine or chemical ducting that needs nickel-alloy corrosion behavior plus geometry freedom, without forcing the part into a more structural nickel category than it actually needs.
