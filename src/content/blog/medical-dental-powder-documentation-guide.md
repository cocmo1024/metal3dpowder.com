Medical and dental AM sourcing fails when the documentation level is treated like a general industrial order. The alloy may be correct, but if the record set does not support traceability, process control, and downstream review, the powder is not ready for the real workflow.

The U.S. FDA's additive-manufactured medical-device guidance is useful here because it emphasizes design, manufacturing, and testing considerations rather than treating AM as a simple extension of conventional production. Powder procurement should follow the same logic.

## Why medical and dental sourcing is different

Medical and dental programs typically need more than a material name and a COA. Buyers often need a package that supports:

- batch traceability
- chemistry confirmation
- PSD confirmation
- process consistency review
- linkage to testing and release workflows

That does not mean every dental lab and every medical-device manufacturer uses the same document set. It means the documentation conversation needs to start earlier and stay more disciplined.

## What buyers should ask for

At minimum, buyers should consider asking for:

- chemistry certificate
- PSD report
- batch or lot traceability
- packaging identity
- production date or release documentation
- safety documentation where required

If the project is moving toward formal submissions or controlled production, the buyer should also align document expectations with internal regulatory and quality teams before release.

## Why cobalt chrome and titanium stay central

Many medical and dental AM sourcing discussions center on cobalt chrome and titanium. That is because the end-use logic often values biocompatibility-related pathways, corrosion behavior, and established manufacturing familiarity.

For site-specific alloy guidance, start with [CoCrMo powder](/posts/Alloys/cocrmo-cocrw-powder/) or [commercially pure titanium powder](/posts/Alloys/ta1-cp-titanium-powder/) if those are the candidate materials.

## What not to do

Do not assume a general industrial document pack is enough for a medical or dental program. Do not assume the supplier can infer traceability expectations from the alloy name alone. And do not leave the quality team out of the RFQ stage if the build is intended for anything beyond basic development work.

## The practical takeaway

Medical and dental AM powder procurement should be treated as a documentation-first sourcing task. The alloy matters, but the real buying quality shows up in traceability, release records, and alignment with the downstream quality pathway.
