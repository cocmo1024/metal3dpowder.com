Prototype powder and production powder are often treated as if they should be quoted from the same specification. That assumption creates confusion, wasted quote cycles, and preventable cost surprises. The reality is simple: the later the program stage, the more precise the powder definition needs to be.

That does not mean production specifications must always be rigid from day one. It means buyers should stop pretending that a trial-build RFQ and a production-support RFQ are the same document.

## What usually changes from prototype to production

As a program matures, buyers typically tighten:

- chemistry expectations
- PSD expectations
- traceability requirements
- document scope
- packaging and handling rules
- reuse policy

The production conversation is usually less tolerant of vague language because the powder has to fit a repeatable process instead of a learning build.

## Why prototype quotes often look cheaper

Prototype-oriented quotes may be lighter on documentation, packaging requirements, and internal release expectations. That can be completely reasonable at an early stage. The problem starts when a buyer compares that quote with a production-ready offer and assumes the supplier simply became more expensive.

In many cases, the quote changed because the specification matured, not because the supplier became less competitive.

## What a prototype RFQ really needs

A prototype RFQ should still define the essential technical scope:

- alloy
- process route
- PSD target
- quantity
- destination

It does not need to pretend the project is already in formal production if that is not true. Good prototype sourcing is honest about stage and intent.

## What production sourcing adds

Production-oriented AM sourcing usually adds more explicit control around:

- incoming inspection expectations
- lot identity and traceability
- certificate requirements
- packaging consistency
- release criteria
- logistics stability

If the project is medical, aerospace, or other controlled end use, this becomes even more important because the powder order has to support a bigger quality system, not just a build.

That transition should be documented, not just assumed. The dedicated [prototype-to-qualification handoff guide](/posts/Alloys/prototype-to-qualification-powder-document-handoff/) is built for the moment when the powder record needs to move from trial logic into controlled release logic.

## Why this matters during quote comparison

Buyers frequently compare a prototype-style quote with a production-style quote and call the second one overpriced. That is usually the wrong diagnosis. The real issue is that the offers describe different program stages.

The right practice is to normalize the specification first, then compare the offers. The companion guide on [comparing two powder quotes](/posts/Alloys/compare-metal-powder-quotes/) is built for that step.

## The practical takeaway

Prototype sourcing should be fast and clear. Production sourcing should be controlled and explicit. Problems start when one is mistaken for the other. The buyer should state the project stage in the RFQ and let the specification maturity follow the real program.
