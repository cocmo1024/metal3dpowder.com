IN718 and Hastelloy X often appear in the same technical discussion because both are hot-side nickel alloys. The mistake is treating them as interchangeable premium nickel options. They usually belong to different hardware problems.

## The shortest decision rule

Choose **IN718** when the part is structurally demanding and temperature-loaded. Choose **Hastelloy X** when the part is more oxidation-driven, hot-gas-facing, or furnace-oriented.

That rule is not universal, but it is the correct first filter for most AM projects.

## Where IN718 usually wins

IN718 is often stronger when the application centers on:

- turbine-adjacent structural parts
- high-strength hot-side brackets or supports
- mechanically demanding nickel hardware
- parts with a more structural qualification history

Its value is tied to strength retention and a very mature post-build route.

## Where Hastelloy X usually wins

Hastelloy X is often stronger when the application centers on:

- furnace hardware
- hot-gas ducting
- combustor-adjacent components
- oxidation-driven thermal service

Its value is tied less to peak structural strength and more to the way it handles oxidation-oriented hot environments.

## Why this comparison matters

Projects lose time when they ask for a hot-side nickel alloy without deciding whether the failure mode is mainly structural or mainly oxidation-driven. IN718 and Hastelloy X solve different versions of that problem. The alloy should match the environment before it matches the preference list.

## The practical takeaway

Use IN718 for turbine-style structural hot-side hardware. Use Hastelloy X for furnace and hot-gas hardware where oxidation resistance sits closer to the center of the decision.
