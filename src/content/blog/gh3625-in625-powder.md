GH3625 is commonly aligned with IN625, a nickel alloy selected first for corrosion resistance and only then for its broader mechanical profile. In additive manufacturing, that changes how buyers evaluate the powder. The question is not only whether the alloy can be printed. The question is whether the finished part can survive the target chemistry and temperature environment.

On this site, IN625 starts from **USD 71/kg**. That places it below IN718 and Hastelloy X in starting price, which is commercially sensible for a nickel alloy often chosen for corrosion-first jobs rather than for the most demanding structural hot-section service.

## Why IN625 is specified

IN625 is frequently used for chloride-bearing environments, chemical process equipment, marine exposure, and oxidation-related service. It is a strong candidate when corrosion resistance is the central requirement and the project still needs a nickel-based AM alloy.

Buyers often arrive at IN625 when:

- stainless steel is no longer chemically sufficient
- the job needs a nickel alloy but not necessarily the default structural route of IN718
- the environment matters more than peak age-hardened strength

That makes IN625 a practical alloy for chemical, marine-adjacent, and corrosion-oriented industrial AM hardware.

## Powder sourcing priorities

PSD, morphology, oxygen, and batch cleanliness all remain important, but the procurement conversation should also include the service environment. A corrosion-driven part may require different supporting documentation than a general development build.

At a minimum, the inquiry should clarify:

- end-use media or environment
- AM process
- PSD range
- quantity
- required documents
- destination country

These points make a large difference to how useful the first quotation is.

## Process and post-processing logic

IN625 is more forgiving than some higher-strength nickel systems, but it still benefits from disciplined build control, heat treatment review, and final inspection planning. Buyers should confirm whether the job is a general engineering part, a corrosive-service component, or a qualification-stage application.

That distinction matters because nickel-alloy procurement can look deceptively simple when the RFQ only states the alloy name. The real price and release logic often sit in the documentation and use-case details, not in the chemistry line alone.

## What to include in the inquiry

- End-use environment
- Target AM process
- Particle size range
- Quantity and package size
- Required documents and destination country

Those details help separate real corrosion-service procurement from generic alloy quoting.

## When IN625 is not the right answer

If the part is primarily structural at elevated temperature, [IN718 powder](/posts/Alloys/gh4169-in718-powder/) may be a better starting point. If the job is more specifically hot-section and oxidation-driven, [Hastelloy X powder](/posts/Alloys/hastelloy-x-powder/) may be worth evaluating. The point is not to buy the most expensive nickel alloy. The point is to match the alloy to the failure mode.
