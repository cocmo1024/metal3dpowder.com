GH3625 is commonly aligned with IN625, a nickel alloy selected first for corrosion resistance and only then for its broader mechanical profile. In additive manufacturing, that changes how buyers evaluate the powder. The question is not only whether the alloy can be printed. The question is whether the finished part can survive the target chemistry and temperature environment.

## Why IN625 is specified

IN625 is frequently used for chloride-bearing environments, chemical process equipment, marine exposure, and oxidation-related service. It is a strong candidate when corrosion resistance is the central requirement and the project still needs a nickel-based AM alloy.

## Powder sourcing priorities

PSD, morphology, oxygen, and batch cleanliness all remain important, but the procurement conversation should also include the service environment. A corrosion-driven part may require different supporting documentation than a general development build.

## Process and post-processing logic

IN625 is more forgiving than some higher-strength nickel systems, but it still benefits from disciplined build control, heat treatment review, and final inspection planning. Buyers should confirm whether the job is a general engineering part, a corrosive-service component, or a qualification-stage application.

## What to include in the inquiry

- End-use environment
- Target AM process
- Particle size range
- Quantity and package size
- Required documents and destination country

Those details help separate real corrosion-service procurement from generic alloy quoting.
