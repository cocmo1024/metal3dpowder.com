Ti64 remains the default aerospace AM powder conversation because it solves a very specific structural problem well: high specific strength in geometries that are difficult to machine or assemble efficiently. That does not make it the answer for every flight part. It means it is often the first serious alloy to evaluate when weight, geometry consolidation, and structural credibility matter at the same time.

## Where Ti64 is usually the right aerospace route

Ti64 becomes practical when the part is:

- weight-driven
- structurally loaded
- geometry-constrained
- difficult to machine as one piece

Typical examples include brackets, housings, supports, manifolds, and other structural hardware where LPBF or EBM can remove fasteners, reduce part count, or improve the stiffness-to-weight result.

## Why aerospace teams stay with Ti64 so often

The material is widely understood, broadly processable, and compatible with the kind of qualification mindset aerospace teams already expect. It is not just the alloy properties. It is also the maturity of the surrounding manufacturing route.

That matters because aerospace programs do not adopt AM materials for novelty. They adopt them when the geometry value and the process discipline justify the transition.

## Where Ti64 is less convincing

Ti64 is not automatically the best answer when the part is:

- primarily corrosion-driven rather than structural
- dominated by extreme hot-section service
- easily machined from wrought stock without part-count penalty

In those cases, the geometry benefit or service condition may point toward a different route. The key decision is not whether titanium is attractive in general. It is whether Ti64 is the right structural titanium route for the exact use case.

## Process fit matters as much as alloy fit

For aerospace structural parts, Ti64 is most often discussed in LPBF, but EBM and DED also enter the picture depending on section size, residual-stress strategy, repair logic, or deposition rate needs.

That is why the application decision should always answer two questions together:

1. is Ti64 the right alloy family for the part
2. is the chosen AM route the right manufacturing path for the geometry

## The practical takeaway

Ti64 is strongest as an aerospace AM powder when the part is structurally loaded, weight-sensitive, and geometrically valuable enough that AM changes the design outcome. If the part is really a structural titanium problem rather than a generic metal problem, Ti64 usually deserves to be the first route reviewed.
