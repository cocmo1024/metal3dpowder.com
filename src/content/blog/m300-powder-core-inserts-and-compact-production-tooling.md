M300 is often the cleaner tooling route when the part is not a broad hot-work tool, but a compact core insert or production tooling component that depends on precision, stiffness, and post-aged strength. That is where maraging logic usually becomes more useful than a hotter-work steel logic.

## Where M300 is usually strongest

This route is commonly evaluated for:

- core inserts
- compact production tooling
- mold-side precision inserts
- tooling components that benefit from internal cooling and stable shape control

The AM value often comes from compact geometry, targeted cooling, and insert redesign rather than from extreme hot-work duty.

## Why core inserts keep pointing to M300

Core inserts are often precision-driven. They may need high post-aged strength, dimensional credibility, and cooling geometry inside a very compact tooling envelope. That is exactly where M300 keeps showing up as a practical AM steel.

## When another steel should take over

If the tool is genuinely punished by hotter duty and repeated thermal cycling, [H13](/posts/Alloys/h13-tool-steel-powder/) may be the more honest answer. The choice depends on whether the insert is primarily a precision tooling problem or a hot-work problem.

## The practical takeaway

Use M300 when the application is a core insert or compact production tooling component that values aged strength, precision, and cooling geometry more than hot-work thermal-fatigue resistance.
