M300 is one of the most commercially useful AM steels for precision tooling. Buyers often come to it when they need high strength, high hardness after aging, and a practical route for mold inserts or conformal cooling components. In additive manufacturing, that combination makes it one of the most widely discussed tooling powders.

On this site, M300 starts from **USD 58/kg**, which is slightly above H13. That pricing position reflects the alloy's role as a specialized tooling material rather than a general structural steel.

## Why M300 is popular in AM

M300 is commonly used for inserts, molds, and tooling geometries where AM delivers clear design value through internal cooling or shape simplification. The material is attractive because the tooling case is often easier to explain commercially than with more exotic alloys: the buyer wants harder, stronger tooling with geometry that conventional machining struggles to deliver.

That makes M300 a frequent fit for:

- injection mold inserts
- conformal cooling components
- precision tooling
- high-strength workshop and production aids

## Why buyers choose it instead of H13

M300 and H13 are not interchangeable. The choice usually depends on the thermal severity of the job.

M300 is often preferred when the project values:

- high strength after aging
- tooling precision
- mold insert performance
- conformal cooling benefits

H13 is often the better answer when the tool will see more aggressive hot-work duty and repeated thermal cycling. That is why the right comparison is usually not "which steel is better" but "which service condition are we really designing for."

## What the RFQ needs to say

If the buyer wants a useful first quote for M300, the inquiry should define:

- tooling type
- PSD requirement
- AM process
- post-build aging expectation
- quantity
- package size
- ship-to country

Without that, the quote may still be technically correct, but it will not separate a quick prototype insert from a production-oriented tooling program.

## Powder quality is not a minor detail

Because M300 is commonly used in tooling, consistency matters. Stable PSD, chemistry control, morphology, and batch traceability all influence whether the material can move from trial builds into repeatable insert production.

That is why "maraging steel powder" alone is not enough as a procurement description. The RFQ needs to communicate the tooling objective and the release expectations.

## Where M300 fits commercially

M300 is usually easier to justify than nickel or titanium powders because the business case is often direct: longer tool life, better cooling, fewer machining constraints, or faster tool iteration. That makes it a strong AM traffic topic because the search intent often sits close to a real purchasing discussion.

## The practical takeaway

Use M300 when the project is tooling-driven and the value comes from high post-aged strength, insert performance, and conformal cooling geometry. Compare it against [H13 tool steel powder](/posts/Alloys/h13-tool-steel-powder/) when the service condition becomes more heat- and thermal-fatigue-driven.
