Most bad powder quotes are caused by bad RFQs. The supplier is asked to quote "Ti64 powder" or "316L powder" with no process, no PSD, no document list, and no destination country. The result is predictable: the first price looks simple, but the conversation becomes slow and the final offer changes later.

An RFQ should do one job well. It should let the supplier quote the real project rather than a generic material name.

## The minimum RFQ structure

At the minimum, every AM powder RFQ should state:

- alloy grade
- target AM process
- particle size range
- quantity
- destination country

That is the baseline. Without those items, most quotes are only placeholders.

## Alloy grade: be specific

The alloy field should be exact whenever possible. "Titanium powder" is not enough. "TC4 / Ti-6Al-4V Grade 23" is much better. The same applies to IN718 versus IN625, 316L versus 17-4PH, and H13 versus M300.

If the alloy is not yet fixed, say that openly and explain the priority. For example, the part may be corrosion-driven, strength-driven, lightweight, or conductivity-driven. That lets the supplier respond with the right options instead of quoting the wrong one quickly.

## Process and PSD must be paired

Particle size requests should never be separated from process type. LPBF, EBM, and DED do not use the same powder cut. A PSD that is normal for LPBF may be wrong for a different route.

If the machine model is known, include it. That does not mean the supplier sets parameters for you. It means the supplier can judge whether the requested powder cut and documentation are commercially reasonable for the machine family.

If the process is LPBF and the buyer still needs help on PSD, the [LPBF particle size guide](/posts/Alloys/lpbf-powder-particle-size-guide/) is the right next step.

## Quantity changes the quotation quality

Suppliers quote differently for:

- lab or trial quantity
- pilot or qualification quantity
- production replenishment

The buyer does not need to know the final annual consumption on day one, but the supplier should know whether the request is for a small development lot or a recurring commercial program.

## Certificates, traceability, and testing

This is where many industrial RFQs stay too vague. If the buyer needs chemistry certificates, PSD reports, oxygen data, batch traceability, or export documents, those items should be listed in the first inquiry.

The same applies to internal inspection rules. A supplier cannot protect the quote against later disputes if the document package is still undefined.

If the buyer is unsure what to request, start with a [COA and certificate review guide](/posts/Alloys/metal-powder-coa-certificate-guide/).

If the shipment is international, add the paperwork scope early as well. The dedicated [export document checklist](/posts/Alloys/export-document-checklist-metal-powder/) helps prevent a technically correct quote from becoming a weak export file.

## Packaging and logistics

Packaging is not a small detail. Container size, inert packing expectations, and shipment destination all affect both cost and lead time. A technically correct quote can still fail commercially if the packaging format does not fit the receiving workflow or the export route.

At the RFQ stage, buyers should include:

- preferred package size
- destination country
- target ship date
- any import or document constraints already known

## Questions that improve the first response

If the supplier is expected to respond with something more useful than a number, the RFQ should also mention:

- whether the part is prototype-only or qualification-relevant
- whether reused powder will be part of the workflow
- whether the key driver is strength, corrosion, temperature, conductivity, or wear
- whether there is an internal oxygen or chemistry ceiling

These are not "nice to have" details. They are the difference between a fast but generic quote and a technically useful one.

If the program is medical, dental, or aerospace-oriented, align the RFQ with the relevant [medical and dental documentation guide](/posts/Alloys/medical-dental-powder-documentation-guide/) or [aerospace qualification checklist](/posts/Alloys/aerospace-powder-qualification-checklist/) before the supplier quotes.

## The practical takeaway

A good RFQ does not need to be long. It needs to be complete enough that the supplier can quote the correct alloy route, the correct PSD window, and the correct document package on the first pass.

If that happens, price comparison becomes meaningful. If it does not, buyers end up comparing placeholders instead of real offers.
