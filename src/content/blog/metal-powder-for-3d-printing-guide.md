Metal powder for 3D printing is not one product category with one correct answer. The right powder depends on alloy family, process route, particle-size window, application risk, and whether the buyer needs powder supply only or a complete manufacturing route.

Most buyers start with a broad query such as "metal powder for 3D printing" or "3D printing metal powder supplier." The useful next step is to narrow the question: what alloy, what process, what particle size, what quantity, and what application?

## Common metal powder families

For additive manufacturing, the most common powder families include:

- titanium powder such as Ti64, Grade 23, Grade 2, and TA15,
- stainless steel powder such as 316L and 17-4PH,
- nickel alloy powder such as IN718, IN625, and Hastelloy X,
- aluminum powder such as AlSi10Mg,
- copper alloy powder such as CuCrZr,
- cobalt chrome powder such as CoCrMo,
- tool steel powder such as H13 and M300.

Each family solves a different problem. Titanium is often strength-to-weight or medical driven. Stainless is usually corrosion, production, or fixture driven. Nickel alloys sit in high-temperature and corrosion-heavy applications. Copper focuses on conductivity and thermal management. Aluminum is lightweight packaging. Tool steels are used for molds, inserts, dies, and production tooling.

## Particle size depends on the process

Particle-size distribution should follow the intended process route. LPBF often uses fine powder cuts such as 15-45 um or 15-53 um depending on alloy and machine preference. EBM may use coarser titanium powder. DED powder and MIM or binder jet feedstocks follow different logic.

The buyer should not ask for a particle size in isolation. The same alloy can be supplied in different PSD windows for different manufacturing routes. A stable inquiry states the process first, then the expected powder window.

## Price is not only alloy cost

Metal 3D printing powder price is affected by alloy chemistry, powder production route, particle-size cut, morphology, oxygen control, lot size, and inspection requirements. Titanium, nickel, copper, aluminum, stainless, and cobalt chrome powders do not price the same way.

For early budgeting, broad published ranges are useful, but formal pricing still depends on grade, quantity, packaging, destination, and certificate requirements. A buyer comparing prices should compare the same alloy, same particle-size window, same acceptance basis, and similar lot quantity.

## What to send before RFQ

A useful RFQ should include:

- alloy or target application,
- process route such as LPBF, EBM, DED, MIM, or binder jet,
- particle-size expectation,
- quantity and delivery country,
- required documents such as COA, PSD report, or chemistry limits,
- whether the powder is for development, qualification, or recurring production.

If the part application is already known, include it. A Ti64 powder inquiry for a lattice bracket is different from a Ti64 inquiry for a medical implant. A 316L powder inquiry for a manifold is different from a 316L inquiry for MIM filters.

## Supplier fit matters, but the product comes first

A good supplier conversation should begin with material fit. The supplier should be able to discuss alloy identity, particle-size range, packaging, lead time, and the application logic behind the powder choice.

Generic supplier claims do not help much. The buyer needs a powder route that matches the part, process, and release expectations.
