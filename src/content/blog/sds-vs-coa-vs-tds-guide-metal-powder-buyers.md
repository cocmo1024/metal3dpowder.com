AM powder buyers often ask for "the documents" as if SDS, COA, and TDS all answer the same question. They do not. Treating them as interchangeable is one of the easiest ways to weaken RFQs, incoming inspection, and internal release decisions.

The basic distinction is straightforward: SDS addresses safe handling and hazards, COA addresses lot-specific measured release data, and TDS usually describes typical product or application information. A serious powder purchase often needs all three, but for different reasons.

## What an SDS is for

An SDS exists to communicate hazard and handling information. It is relevant to storage, workplace precautions, and safe movement of the material. That makes it operationally important, but it does not prove that a specific lot matches a purchasing specification.

An SDS can help answer:

- what hazards and handling controls need attention
- what storage and response precautions apply
- what the receiving and EHS teams should understand before use

It does not answer whether the lot chemistry, PSD, or traceability are correct for the project.

## What a COA is for

A COA is the document buyers usually rely on when they need lot-specific release evidence. It is the document most likely to speak directly to:

- chemistry results
- oxygen or interstitial data where relevant
- PSD results
- lot identity
- release references

This is why COA belongs at the center of incoming inspection and approval logic. The existing [COA and certificate guide](/posts/Alloys/metal-powder-coa-certificate-guide/) goes deeper into what that review should look like.

## What a TDS is for

A TDS is generally the place for typical product information, application framing, or nominal material data. That can be useful for early evaluation, alloy comparison, or internal orientation, but it should not be mistaken for lot release evidence.

A TDS can support:

- early alloy screening
- typical process or product understanding
- internal discussion before the RFQ is finalized

It should not replace a COA when the actual lot is being released for use.

## Why buyers confuse them

The confusion usually happens because all three documents are attached to the same product conversation. But they serve different functions:

1. SDS helps the team handle the powder safely
2. COA helps the team decide whether the specific lot is releasable
3. TDS helps the team understand the product in a more general sense

If a buyer uses one document to answer all three questions, the release process becomes weaker immediately.

## What to ask for in a serious RFQ

If the powder order matters commercially, the RFQ should be explicit about which documents are required and why. That usually means defining:

- whether SDS is needed for internal handling and receiving
- what COA content is expected for release
- whether TDS is being requested only for general review

That level of clarity makes supplier responses cleaner and keeps the document package aligned with the actual workflow.

## The practical takeaway

SDS, COA, and TDS should be treated as complementary documents, not substitutes. Buyers who keep those functions separate usually get cleaner RFQs, better incoming inspection discipline, and fewer internal release arguments. The most important rule is simple: use the COA for the lot, use the SDS for the hazard, and use the TDS for the general product picture.
