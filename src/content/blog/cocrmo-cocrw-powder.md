Cobalt-chromium alloy powders are chosen when parts need to resist wear, corrosion, or both under demanding service conditions. In additive manufacturing, CoCrMo and CoCrW remain relevant for medical, industrial, and tribology-driven applications where stainless steel may not be sufficient.

On this site, CoCrMo starts from **USD 84/kg**, which reflects its position as a high-value specialty powder rather than a general engineering alloy.

## Why CoCr systems stay relevant

Their value comes from a combination of passivation, hot hardness, and wear-related microstructural behavior. That makes them suitable for parts facing sliding contact, corrosive media, or surface-durability requirements.

That relevance shows up in three common AM conversations:

- dental and medical hardware
- industrial wear components
- parts that need a stronger wear/corrosion answer than stainless steel

The buyer usually comes to cobalt chrome because the service condition has already ruled out easier metals.

## Powder concerns for procurement

As with other high-value AM powders, buyers should confirm PSD, morphology, chemistry, and batch traceability. CoCr systems are often chosen for high-consequence applications, so documentation and consistency typically matter more than headline pricing alone.

For cobalt chrome, supporting documents can matter as much as the alloy name because the end use is often application-sensitive. A development part, a dental framework program, and an industrial wear component do not always need the same document package.

## Before you send the RFQ

Clarify whether the project is medical, industrial wear service, or corrosion-driven hardware. The performance target changes the useful procurement conversation more than the alloy family name by itself.

The most useful RFQs normally include:

- intended application
- AM process
- PSD range
- quantity
- documentation scope
- destination country

That information turns a high-value specialty powder inquiry into something a supplier can quote accurately.

## Why the alloy family alone is not enough

Buyers sometimes request "cobalt chrome powder" as if that is already a complete specification. It is not. In practice, the useful procurement question is whether the job is driven by wear, corrosion, medical handling, or a combination of those factors. That affects both the technical discussion and the document package.

With CoCr systems, the alloy name gets attention, but the application logic closes the deal. A serious inquiry explains the part function, the AM route, and the reason cobalt chrome is being considered instead of stainless steel or titanium.
